# Synchro-Beam Longitudinal Kick

This note derives the six-dimensional longitudinal kick for a particle
colliding with a thin Gaussian source slice. It treats the moving source
centroid, the complete transported transverse covariance including
$\sigma_{xy}$, the rotation of the covariance principal axes, and the
slingshot effect generated by the virtual drift.

The derivation applies to both weak-strong and sliced soft-Gaussian
strong-strong models. No particular software representation is assumed.

## 1. Assumptions and conventions

Use canonical accelerator coordinates

$$
    \mathbf q=(x,p_x,y,p_y,z,p_z),
    \qquad
    z=s-\ell,
    \qquad
    p_z=\frac{P-P_0}{P_0}.
$$

The derivation assumes:

1. ultrarelativistic, counter-propagating beams;
2. a thin source slice at the collision point;
3. a two-dimensional transverse potential for each source slice;
4. no dissipative process during the collision; and
5. canonical pairs $(x,p_x)$, $(y,p_y)$, and $(z,p_z)$.

All charge, energy, slice-population, and normalization factors are included
in the potential $U$. Define the transverse kick by

$$
    \mathbf F=
    \begin{pmatrix}F_x\\F_y\end{pmatrix}
    =-\nabla_{\mathbf r}U,
    \qquad
    \mathbf r=\begin{pmatrix}x\\y\end{pmatrix}.
$$

The source travels along an axis opposite to that of the field particle. If
$S$ is the distance from the interaction point to the collision point in
the field-particle axis, then

$$
    u=-S
$$

is the same collision-point location in the source axis. Derivatives with
respect to $u$ will be denoted by a subscript $u$.

## 2. Collision-point geometry

Let $z$ be the longitudinal coordinate of the field particle at the
interaction point and $z_*$ the longitudinal coordinate of the source
slice in its own axis. If both travel at the speed of light, their arrival
times at a point $S$ satisfy

$$
    \frac{S-z}{c}=\frac{-S-z_*}{c}.
$$

Solving gives

$$
    S=\frac{z-z_*}{2},
    \qquad
    \frac{\partial S}{\partial z}=\frac12,
    \qquad
    u=-\frac{z-z_*}{2},
    \qquad
    \frac{\partial u}{\partial z}=-\frac12.
$$

The synchro-beam map is the composition

$$
    \mathcal M=\mathcal D^{-1}\mathcal B\mathcal D,
$$

where $\mathcal D$ is a canonical virtual drift from the interaction point
to the collision point, $\mathcal B$ is the thin beam-beam kick there, and
$\mathcal D^{-1}$ returns the particle to the interaction point.

A virtual drift is not an ordinary drift with a different externally fixed
length for every particle. Its length depends on the canonical coordinate
$z$, so a compensating $p_z$ transformation is required for
symplecticity. This is the defining construction of the synchro-beam map in
Ref. [1].

## 3. Canonical origin of the virtual-drift energy change

Use the Poisson bracket

$$
    [f,g]
    =
    \sum_{w=x,y,z}
    \left(
      \frac{\partial f}{\partial w}
      \frac{\partial g}{\partial p_w}
      -
      \frac{\partial f}{\partial p_w}
      \frac{\partial g}{\partial w}
    \right).
$$

For a momentum-only drift Hamiltonian
$H(p_x,p_y,p_z)$, define

$$
    h=-S(z,z_*)H,
    \qquad
    \mathcal D=\exp(:h:).
$$

The first Poisson brackets are

$$
\begin{aligned}
    :h:x
      &=[h,x]
       =S\frac{\partial H}{\partial p_x},\\
    :h:y
      &=[h,y]
       =S\frac{\partial H}{\partial p_y},\\
    :h:z
      &=[h,z]
       =S\frac{\partial H}{\partial p_z},\\
    :h:p_z
      &=[h,p_z]
       =-\frac{\partial S}{\partial z}H.
\end{aligned}
$$

The last equation is the origin of the virtual-drift longitudinal momentum
change. If $H$ is independent of $p_z$, the Lie series terminates after
the first bracket. If $H$ depends on $p_z$, higher brackets generate the
modified transformations derived in Section 7.

## 4. Gaussian potential with a general transverse covariance

Let the source centroid and covariance at source-axis position $u$ be

$$
    \mathbf C(u)
      =
      \begin{pmatrix}C_x(u)\\C_y(u)\end{pmatrix},
    \qquad
    A(u)
      =
      \begin{pmatrix}
        a(u) & b(u)\\
        b(u) & d(u)
      \end{pmatrix},
$$

where

$$
    a=\sigma_x^2,
    \qquad
    b=\sigma_{xy},
    \qquad
    d=\sigma_y^2.
$$

The normalized Gaussian density is

$$
    \rho(\mathbf r';\mathbf C,A)
      =
      \frac{
        \exp\!\left[
          -\frac12
          (\mathbf r'-\mathbf C)^T
          A^{-1}
          (\mathbf r'-\mathbf C)
        \right]
      }{
        2\pi\sqrt{\det A}
      }.
$$

Let $G$ be the two-dimensional Green function and let $\kappa$ contain
the physical beam-beam normalization. The source potential is

$$
    U(\mathbf r;\mathbf C,A)
      =
      \kappa
      \int_{\mathbb R^2}
      G(\mathbf r-\mathbf r')
      \rho(\mathbf r';\mathbf C,A)
      \,d^2\mathbf r'.
$$

In the covariance principal axes, with eigenvalues
$\lambda_1,\lambda_2$ and relative coordinates
$\widehat x,\widehat y$, choose a fixed positive scale $t_0$. A convergent
integral representation of the same potential is

$$
    U
      =
      \kappa
      \int_0^\infty
      \left\{
        \frac{
          \exp\!\left[
            -\frac{\widehat x^2}{2(\lambda_1+t)}
            -\frac{\widehat y^2}{2(\lambda_2+t)}
          \right]
        }{
          \sqrt{(\lambda_1+t)(\lambda_2+t)}
        }
        -
        \frac{1}{t+t_0}
      \right\}
      \,dt.
$$

The subtraction fixes the logarithmic-potential gauge. Because $t_0$ is
independent of position, centroid, covariance, and collision location, it
does not affect any kick or derivative. A gauge term that depends on $u$
would change the longitudinal kick and is therefore not permissible.

Differentiating this potential with respect to
$\widehat x,\widehat y$ gives the Bassetti-Erskine transverse field.
The longitudinal derivation below does not depend on which numerically
equivalent form of that transverse field is used. The closed complex-error
function representation is derived in Ref. [3].

### 4.1 Covariance derivative identity

A Gaussian obeys the matrix heat equation. For an arbitrary symmetric
covariance variation $dA$,

$$
    d\rho
      =
      \frac12
      (dA):\nabla_{\mathbf r'}\nabla_{\mathbf r'}\rho,
$$

where the Frobenius contraction is

$$
    X:Y=\operatorname{tr}(X^TY).
$$

To obtain the corresponding potential variation, substitute the heat
equation into the convolution:

$$
\begin{aligned}
    dU
      &=
      \kappa
      \int G(\mathbf r-\mathbf r')\,d\rho\,d^2\mathbf r'\\
      &=
      \frac{\kappa}{2}
      (dA):
      \int
      G(\mathbf r-\mathbf r')
      \nabla_{\mathbf r'}\nabla_{\mathbf r'}\rho
      \,d^2\mathbf r'.
\end{aligned}
$$

Integrating by parts twice moves both derivatives from $\rho$ to $G$.
Boundary terms vanish for the Gaussian density. Since two derivatives remove
the sign introduced by
$\nabla_{\mathbf r'}G(\mathbf r-\mathbf r')=-\nabla_{\mathbf r}G(\mathbf r-\mathbf r')$,
the result is

$$
    dU
      =
      \frac12 H_U:dA,
    \qquad
    H_U=\nabla_{\mathbf r}\nabla_{\mathbf r}U.
$$

In laboratory coordinates,

$$
    H_U
      =
      \begin{pmatrix}
        U_{xx} & U_{xy}\\
        U_{xy} & U_{yy}
      \end{pmatrix},
$$

so

$$
    H_U:dA
      =
      U_{xx}\,da
      +2U_{xy}\,db
      +U_{yy}\,dd.
$$

The factor of two multiplying the $\sigma_{xy}$ variation is required
because both symmetric off-diagonal entries of $A$ vary together.

### 4.2 Complete direct collision-point longitudinal kick

At fixed field-particle collision-point coordinate, define the relative
coordinate

$$
    \boldsymbol\xi
      =
      \mathbf r_{\mathrm{CP}}-\mathbf C(u).
$$

Its source-axis derivative is

$$
    \boldsymbol\xi_u=-\mathbf C_u.
$$

Because $\nabla_{\boldsymbol\xi}U=-\mathbf F$, the centroid contribution
to the potential derivative is

$$
    \frac{dU}{du}\bigg|_{\mathrm{centroid}}
      =
      \nabla_{\boldsymbol\xi}U\cdot\boldsymbol\xi_u
      =
      (-\mathbf F)\cdot(-\mathbf C_u)
      =
      \mathbf F\cdot\mathbf C_u.
$$

Adding the covariance variation gives

$$
    \frac{dU}{du}
      =
      \mathbf F\cdot\mathbf C_u
      +
      \frac12 H_U:A_u.
$$

The thin canonical kick changes longitudinal momentum by

$$
    \Delta p_z^{\mathrm{CP}}
      =
      -\frac{\partial U}{\partial z}.
$$

Using $u_z=-1/2$ therefore gives the central result:

$$
\boxed{
    \Delta p_z^{\mathrm{CP}}
      =
      \frac12\mathbf F\cdot\mathbf C_u
      +
      \frac14 H_U:A_u.
}
$$

In components,

$$
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
    \Delta p_z^{\mathrm{CP}}
      ={}&
      \frac12
      \left(
        F_x C_{x,u}
        +
        F_y C_{y,u}
      \right)\\
      &+
      \frac14
      \left(
        U_{xx}a_u
        +
        2U_{xy}b_u
        +
        U_{yy}d_u
      \right).
\end{aligned}
}
$$

This expression is coordinate invariant and includes the full
$\sigma_{xy}(u)$ dependence.

## 5. Equivalent principal-axis and rotation formula

The rotation construction in this section is the covariance-matrix form of
the coupled six-dimensional beam-beam treatment in Ref. [4].

Diagonalize the covariance as

$$
    A=R(\theta)\Lambda R^T(\theta),
    \qquad
    \Lambda=
    \begin{pmatrix}\lambda_1&0\\0&\lambda_2\end{pmatrix},
$$

with

$$
    R(\theta)
      =
      \begin{pmatrix}
        \cos\theta&-\sin\theta\\
        \sin\theta& \cos\theta
      \end{pmatrix}.
$$

The relative coordinate and force in the principal frame are

$$
    \widehat{\boldsymbol\xi}=R^T\boldsymbol\xi,
    \qquad
    \widehat{\mathbf F}=R^T\mathbf F.
$$

For

$$
    A=
    \begin{pmatrix}a&b\\b&d\end{pmatrix},
$$

define

$$
    D=\sqrt{(a-d)^2+4b^2}.
$$

The eigenvalues follow from
$\det(A-\lambda I)=0$:

$$
    \lambda_{1,2}
      =
      \frac{a+d\pm D}{2}.
$$

The rotation angle follows by setting the off-diagonal entry of
$R^TAR$ to zero:

$$
    \theta
      =
      \frac12\operatorname{atan2}(2b,a-d).
$$

Differentiating gives

$$
    D_u
      =
      \frac{
        (a-d)(a_u-d_u)+4bb_u
      }{D},
$$

$$
    \lambda_{1,u}
      =
      \frac{a_u+d_u+D_u}{2},
    \qquad
    \lambda_{2,u}
      =
      \frac{a_u+d_u-D_u}{2},
$$

and

$$
    \theta_u
      =
      \frac{
        (a-d)b_u-b(a_u-d_u)
      }{D^2}.
$$

To transform the covariance derivative, define

$$
    J=
    \begin{pmatrix}0&-1\\1&0\end{pmatrix},
    \qquad
    R_u=R\,J\,\theta_u.
$$

Differentiating $A=R\Lambda R^T$ and rotating back to the principal frame
gives

$$
\begin{aligned}
    R^TA_uR
      &=
      \Lambda_u
      +
      \theta_u(J\Lambda-\Lambda J)\\
      &=
      \begin{pmatrix}
        \lambda_{1,u}
        &
        (\lambda_1-\lambda_2)\theta_u\\
        (\lambda_1-\lambda_2)\theta_u
        &
        \lambda_{2,u}
      \end{pmatrix}.
\end{aligned}
$$

Let

$$
    \widehat H_U=R^TH_UR.
$$

Write the entries of this matrix as $\widehat U_{ij}$.
The invariant contraction becomes

$$
\begin{aligned}
    H_U:A_u
      ={}&
      \widehat U_{11}\lambda_{1,u}
      +
      \widehat U_{22}\lambda_{2,u}\\
      &+
      2\widehat U_{12}
      (\lambda_1-\lambda_2)\theta_u.
\end{aligned}
$$

The mixed-Hessian term can be written using the transverse force. At fixed
laboratory coordinate,

$$
    \frac{\partial\widehat{\boldsymbol\xi}}{\partial\theta}
      =
      \begin{pmatrix}
        \widehat y\\
        -\widehat x
      \end{pmatrix}.
$$

Consequently,

$$
\begin{aligned}
    \frac{\partial U}{\partial\theta}
      &=
      \frac{\partial U}{\partial\widehat x}\widehat y
      -
      \frac{\partial U}{\partial\widehat y}\widehat x\\
      &=
      -\widehat F_x\widehat y
      +
      \widehat F_y\widehat x.
\end{aligned}
$$

The covariance heat identity applied to a pure rotation also gives

$$
    \frac{\partial U}{\partial\theta}
      =
      (\lambda_1-\lambda_2)\widehat U_{12}.
$$

Therefore,

$$
    (\lambda_1-\lambda_2)\widehat U_{12}
      =
      -\widehat F_x\widehat y
      +
      \widehat F_y\widehat x.
$$

Substitution into the direct longitudinal kick yields

$$
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
    \Delta p_z^{\mathrm{CP}}
      ={}&
      \frac12\mathbf F\cdot\mathbf C_u\\
      &+
      \frac14
      \left(
        \widehat U_{11}\lambda_{1,u}
        +
        \widehat U_{22}\lambda_{2,u}
      \right)\\
      &-
      \frac12\theta_u
      \left(
        \widehat F_x\widehat y
        -
        \widehat F_y\widehat x
      \right).
\end{aligned}
}
$$

The three lines are respectively the moving-centroid, principal-size, and
principal-axis rotation contributions.

A transverse model may correctly rotate the coordinates and forces while
still omitting the last longitudinal term. Such a map includes the value of
$\sigma_{xy}$ in the transverse kick but not its collision-position
derivative. It is complete only when $\theta_u=0$, or when the displayed
rotation factor happens to vanish.

When $D=0$, the covariance is exactly round and the principal-axis angle is
undefined. The invariant laboratory-frame formula
$H_U:A_u$ remains finite and must be used directly or through a continuous
round-beam limit.

## 6. Paraxial Hirata drift and the slingshot effect

The original synchro-beam map uses

$$
    H_{\mathrm{par}}
      =
      \frac{p_x^2+p_y^2}{2}.
$$

Since $H_{\mathrm{par}}$ is independent of $p_z$, the first brackets in
Section 3 give the complete forward virtual drift:

$$
\begin{aligned}
    x^-&=x+Sp_x,
    &p_x^-&=p_x,\\
    y^-&=y+Sp_y,
    &p_y^-&=p_y,\\
    z^-&=z,
    &p_z^-&=p_z-\frac{p_x^2+p_y^2}{4}.
\end{aligned}
$$

At the collision point, write

$$
    p_x^+=p_x^-+F_x,
    \qquad
    p_y^+=p_y^-+F_y,
$$

and add the direct longitudinal kick
$\Delta p_z^{\mathrm{CP}}$. The inverse virtual drift uses the post-kick
transverse momenta:

$$
\begin{aligned}
    x_{\mathrm{out}}&=x^--Sp_x^+,
    &p_{x,\mathrm{out}}&=p_x^+,\\
    y_{\mathrm{out}}&=y^--Sp_y^+,
    &p_{y,\mathrm{out}}&=p_y^+,\\
    z_{\mathrm{out}}&=z^-,
    &p_{z,\mathrm{out}}
      &=p_z^-+\Delta p_z^{\mathrm{CP}}
        +\frac{(p_x^+)^2+(p_y^+)^2}{4}.
\end{aligned}
$$

The forward and inverse drift contributions combine into

$$
\boxed{
    \Delta p_z^{\mathrm{sling}}
      =
      \frac{
        (p_x^+)^2+(p_y^+)^2
        -(p_x^-)^2-(p_y^-)^2
      }{4}.
}
$$

Expanding $p_x^+=p_x^-+F_x$ and
$p_y^+=p_y^-+F_y$ gives

$$
\boxed{
    \Delta p_z^{\mathrm{sling}}
      =
      \frac12
      \left(
        p_x^-F_x+p_y^-F_y
      \right)
      +
      \frac14
      \left(
        F_x^2+F_y^2
      \right).
}
$$

The complete paraxial longitudinal change is therefore

$$
\boxed{
    \Delta p_z
      =
      \Delta p_z^{\mathrm{sling}}
      +
      \frac12\mathbf F\cdot\mathbf C_u
      +
      \frac14 H_U:A_u.
}
$$

## 7. Alternative named virtual-drift Hamiltonians

The word “slingshot” describes the energy exchange produced by the complete
forward-drift, transverse-kick, inverse-drift composition. Its algebraic form
depends on the drift Hamiltonian. The quadratic expression in Section 6 is
specific to the paraxial Hirata model. The chromatic and exact-Hamiltonian
virtual drifts below are derived in Ref. [2].

The source element selects this physics through the descriptive
`virtual_drift` convention:

| Convention | Meaning |
|---|---|
| `:hirata` | Paraxial Hirata Hamiltonian $H_{\mathrm{par}}$; this is the default. |
| `:chromatic` | Chromatic Hamiltonian $H_{\mathrm{chr}}$. |
| `:exact` | Exact field-free Hamiltonian $H_{\mathrm{ex}}$. |

Only these three Hamiltonian virtual drifts belong to the physical interface.
Two historical, deliberately incomplete approximations remain available for
regression diagnostics only:

| Explicit unsafe construction | Meaning |
|---|---|
| `UnsafeVirtualDrift(:paraxial_frozen_longitudinal)` | Paraxial transverse displacement with both $z$ and $p_z$ artificially frozen. |
| `UnsafeVirtualDrift(:chromatic_frozen_energy)` | Chromatic transverse and $z$ displacement with the virtual-drift $p_z$ change artificially suppressed. |

The unsafe maps suppress part of the canonical longitudinal response. The
paraxial variant is reversible without a collision but is not symplectic; the
chromatic variant is neither symplectic nor exactly reversible. Neither may be
selected by a plain `virtual_drift` symbol, and neither should be used for
physical tracking.

The direct collision-point formula in Sections 4-5 used
$u=-(z-z_*)/2$ and $u_z=-1/2$. When a modified virtual drift changes $z$
during the IP-to-CP transformation, one must state how the source field is
selected. The formulas below derive the drift maps and their isolated
slingshot terms exactly. Combining them with the direct Gaussian term assumes
the usual frozen-collision-field approximation: the source potential is
evaluated at the collision location selected before the small drift-generated
$z$ variation, so its explicit longitudinal derivative remains the one
derived in Section 4. A model that instead lets the source potential depend
on the modified final collision coordinate must differentiate that complete
dependence anew; the interaction is then not represented by the same
coordinate-only thin kick.

Use

$$
    P=1+p_z,
    \qquad
    q=p_x^2+p_y^2.
$$

### 7.1 Chromatic virtual drift

The chromatic Hamiltonian is

$$
    H_{\mathrm{chr}}=\frac{q}{2P}.
$$

Along the Lie flow parameter $\tau$, the transverse momenta remain constant
and

$$
    \frac{dP}{d\tau}
      =
      -\frac{\partial S}{\partial z}H_{\mathrm{chr}}
      =
      -\frac{q}{4P}.
$$

Hence

$$
    \frac{dP^2}{d\tau}=-\frac q2,
$$

and integration from $\tau=0$ to $1$ gives

$$
    P^-=\sqrt{P^2-\frac q2}.
$$

Define

$$
    \Phi=\frac{P^-}{P}-1
         =\sqrt{1-\frac{q}{2P^2}}-1.
$$

The remaining Lie-flow equations are

$$
    \frac{dx}{d\tau}=\frac{Sp_x}{P},
    \qquad
    \frac{dy}{d\tau}=\frac{Sp_y}{P},
    \qquad
    \frac{dz}{d\tau}=-\frac{Sq}{2P^2}.
$$

Since $S=(z-z_*)/2$,

$$
    \frac{dS}{d\tau}
      =
      -\frac{Sq}{4P^2}.
$$

Comparison with $dP/d\tau=-q/(4P)$ shows

$$
    \frac{d}{d\tau}\left(\frac SP\right)=0.
$$

Therefore $S/P$ is constant during the Lie flow, and direct integration
gives

$$
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
    x^-&=x+\frac{Sp_x}{P},
    &p_x^-&=p_x,\\
    y^-&=y+\frac{Sp_y}{P},
    &p_y^-&=p_y,\\
    z^-&=z+2S\Phi,
    &p_z^-&=p_z+P\Phi.
\end{aligned}
}
$$

For the inverse map, all quantities below are evaluated at its input. Define

$$
    \Psi=\sqrt{1+\frac{q}{2P^2}}-1.
$$

Reversing the Lie flow gives

$$
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
    x_{\mathrm{out}}&=x-\frac{Sp_x}{P},
    &p_{x,\mathrm{out}}&=p_x,\\
    y_{\mathrm{out}}&=y-\frac{Sp_y}{P},
    &p_{y,\mathrm{out}}&=p_y,\\
    z_{\mathrm{out}}&=z+2S\Psi,
    &p_{z,\mathrm{out}}&=p_z+P\Psi.
\end{aligned}
}
$$

If the direct collision-point longitudinal kick is temporarily set to zero,
the forward map gives

$$
    (P^-)^2=P_{\mathrm{in}}^2-\frac{q^-}{2},
$$

and the inverse map after the transverse kick gives

$$
    P_{\mathrm{out}}^2=(P^-)^2+\frac{q^+}{2}.
$$

Thus the chromatic slingshot is

$$
\boxed{
    \Delta p_z^{\mathrm{sling,chr}}
      =
      \sqrt{
        P_{\mathrm{in}}^2+\frac{q^+-q^-}{2}
      }
      -
      P_{\mathrm{in}}.
}
$$

At lowest order in transverse momentum,

$$
    \Delta p_z^{\mathrm{sling,chr}}
      =
      \frac{q^+-q^-}{4P_{\mathrm{in}}}
      +
      O\!\left((q^+-q^-)^2\right).
$$

When the direct collision-point kick is nonzero, it must be inserted between
the two nonlinear maps. It is not generally additive with the isolated
chromatic slingshot formula because the inverse map depends on its input
$p_z$.

### 7.2 Exact-Hamiltonian virtual drift

The exact field-free drift Hamiltonian is

$$
    H_{\mathrm{ex}}
      =
      P-p_s,
    \qquad
    p_s=\sqrt{P^2-p_x^2-p_y^2}.
$$

Let initial interaction-point variables carry a subscript $0$. During a
physical drift over distance $S$,

$$
\begin{aligned}
    x_{\mathrm{CP}}
      &=x_0+\frac{p_{x,0}}{p_{s,0}}S,\\
    y_{\mathrm{CP}}
      &=y_0+\frac{p_{y,0}}{p_{s,0}}S,\\
    z_{\mathrm{CP}}
      &=z_0-\frac{H_{\mathrm{ex},0}}{p_{s,0}}S.
\end{aligned}
$$

The collision condition is

$$
    z_{\mathrm{CP}}=z_*+2S.
$$

Combining the preceding two equations gives

$$
    S
      =
      \frac{(z_0-z_*)p_{s,0}}
           {2p_{s,0}+H_{\mathrm{ex},0}}.
$$

A type-3 generating function for the forward transformation is

$$
    G_3
      =
      -x_{\mathrm{CP}}p_{x,0}
      -y_{\mathrm{CP}}p_{y,0}
      -z_{\mathrm{CP}}p_{z,0}
      +
      H_{\mathrm{ex},0}S(z_{\mathrm{CP}},z_*).
$$

The canonical relations

$$
    x_0=-\frac{\partial G_3}{\partial p_{x,0}},
    \quad
    y_0=-\frac{\partial G_3}{\partial p_{y,0}},
    \quad
    z_0=-\frac{\partial G_3}{\partial p_{z,0}},
    \quad
    p_z=-\frac{\partial G_3}{\partial z_{\mathrm{CP}}}
$$

produce the forward map

$$
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
    x^-&=x_0+\frac{p_{x,0}}{p_{s,0}}S,
    &p_x^-&=p_{x,0},\\
    y^-&=y_0+\frac{p_{y,0}}{p_{s,0}}S,
    &p_y^-&=p_{y,0},\\
    z^-&=z_*+2S,
    &p_z^-&=p_{z,0}-\frac12H_{\mathrm{ex},0}.
\end{aligned}
}
$$

To derive the inverse, let $P=1+p_z$ and $q=p_x^2+p_y^2$ at the inverse
input. The forward momentum relation implies

$$
    P=\frac{P_0+p_{s,0}}{2}.
$$

Also,

$$
    q=P_0^2-p_{s,0}^2
      =(P_0-p_{s,0})(P_0+p_{s,0}).
$$

Therefore

$$
    H_{\mathrm r}
      \equiv
      P_0-p_{s,0}
      =
      \frac{q}{2P},
$$

and

$$
    P_0=P+\frac12H_{\mathrm r},
    \qquad
    p_{s,0}=P-\frac12H_{\mathrm r}.
$$

With

$$
    S=\frac{z-z_*}{2},
$$

the inverse map is

$$
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
    x_{\mathrm{out}}
      &=x-\frac{p_x}{p_{s,0}}S,
    &p_{x,\mathrm{out}}&=p_x,\\
    y_{\mathrm{out}}
      &=y-\frac{p_y}{p_{s,0}}S,
    &p_{y,\mathrm{out}}&=p_y,\\
    z_{\mathrm{out}}
      &=z+\frac{H_{\mathrm r}}{p_{s,0}}S,
    &p_{z,\mathrm{out}}
      &=p_z+\frac12H_{\mathrm r}.
\end{aligned}
}
$$

For an isolated transverse kick with no direct collision-point $p_z$ kick,
define

$$
    p_s^-=\sqrt{P_{\mathrm{in}}^2-q^-},
    \qquad
    P_{\mathrm{CP}}=\frac{P_{\mathrm{in}}+p_s^-}{2}.
$$

After the transverse kick, the inverse adds
$q^+/(4P_{\mathrm{CP}})$. The exact-Hamiltonian slingshot is therefore

$$
\boxed{
    \Delta p_z^{\mathrm{sling,ex}}
      =
      P_{\mathrm{CP}}
      +
      \frac{q^+}{4P_{\mathrm{CP}}}
      -
      P_{\mathrm{in}}.
}
$$

When $q^+=q^-$, the identity

$$
    q^-
      =
      (P_{\mathrm{in}}-p_s^-)
      (P_{\mathrm{in}}+p_s^-)
$$

shows that the slingshot vanishes exactly. Its leading expansion is again

$$
    \Delta p_z^{\mathrm{sling,ex}}
      =
      \frac{q^+-q^-}{4P_{\mathrm{in}}}
      +
      O(q^2).
$$

As in the chromatic model, a nonzero direct collision-point longitudinal kick
must be inserted before evaluating the nonlinear inverse map.

## 8. Weak-strong specialization

For a rigid strong source, write its centroid in its own axis as

$$
    \mathbf C(u)
      =
      \mathbf C_0
      +
      \overline{\mathbf p}_0u
      +
      \frac12\mathbf C_2u^2.
$$

Therefore

$$
    \mathbf C_u
      =
      \overline{\mathbf p}_0+\mathbf C_2u
      =
      \overline{\mathbf p}_0-\mathbf C_2S.
$$

This proves that a constant offset $\mathbf C_0$ changes the transverse
force but produces no separate longitudinal term. A source angle
$\overline{\mathbf p}_0$ and curvature $\mathbf C_2$ do produce one.

Let source transverse phase-space coordinates be

$$
    \mathbf r(u)=\mathbf r_0+\mathbf p_0u.
$$

Define the interaction-point covariance blocks

$$
\begin{aligned}
    A_0&=\operatorname{Cov}(\mathbf r_0,\mathbf r_0),\\
    B_0&=\operatorname{Cov}(\mathbf r_0,\mathbf p_0),\\
    Q_0&=\operatorname{Cov}(\mathbf p_0,\mathbf p_0).
\end{aligned}
$$

Taking the covariance of
$\mathbf r_0+\mathbf p_0u$ gives

$$
    A(u)
      =
      A_0
      +
      u(B_0+B_0^T)
      +
      u^2Q_0.
$$

Differentiating yields

$$
    A_u
      =
      B_0+B_0^T+2uQ_0.
$$

The complete paraxial weak-strong longitudinal kick is consequently

$$
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
    \Delta p_z
      ={}&
      \Delta p_z^{\mathrm{sling}}\\
      &+
      \frac12
      \mathbf F\cdot
      \left(
        \overline{\mathbf p}_0+\mathbf C_2u
      \right)\\
      &+
      \frac14
      H_U:
      \left(
        B_0+B_0^T+2uQ_0
      \right),
      \qquad u=-S.
\end{aligned}
}
$$

### 8.1 Explicit coupling term

The transported off-diagonal position covariance is

$$
\begin{aligned}
    b(u)
      ={}&
      \operatorname{Cov}(x,y)_0\\
      &+
      u\left[
        \operatorname{Cov}(p_x,y)_0
        +
        \operatorname{Cov}(x,p_y)_0
      \right]\\
      &+
      u^2\operatorname{Cov}(p_x,p_y)_0.
\end{aligned}
$$

Hence

$$
    b_u
      =
      \operatorname{Cov}(p_x,y)_0
      +
      \operatorname{Cov}(x,p_y)_0
      +
      2u\operatorname{Cov}(p_x,p_y)_0.
$$

Its direct longitudinal contribution is

$$
\boxed{
    \Delta p_z^{\sigma_{xy}}
      =
      \frac12U_{xy}b_u.
}
$$

This term is exactly equivalent to the combination of the
$\lambda_{1,u}$, $\lambda_{2,u}$, and $\theta_u$ dependencies in the
principal-axis formula. Keeping only transported eigenvalues while omitting
the changing rotation angle is not equivalent to retaining $b_u$.

### 8.2 Uncoupled Twiss limit

For one uncoupled plane,

$$
    \operatorname{Var}(x)=\epsilon\beta,
    \qquad
    \operatorname{Cov}(x,p_x)=-\epsilon\alpha,
    \qquad
    \operatorname{Var}(p_x)=\epsilon\gamma.
$$

Therefore

$$
    \sigma_x^2(u)
      =
      \epsilon
      \left(
        \beta-2\alpha u+\gamma u^2
      \right).
$$

Since $u=-S$,

$$
    \sigma_x^2(S)
      =
      \epsilon
      \left(
        \beta+2\alpha S+\gamma S^2
      \right),
$$

and

$$
    \frac{d\sigma_x^2}{du}
      =
      -2\epsilon(\alpha+\gamma S).
$$

The covariance contribution becomes

$$
    \frac14U_{xx}\frac{d\sigma_x^2}{du}
      =
      -\frac12
      U_{xx}\epsilon(\alpha+\gamma S).
$$

Applying the same result in $y$, and writing
$\mathbf C_2=(p'_{xo},p'_{yo})^T$, gives

$$
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
    \Delta p_z
      ={}&
      \Delta p_z^{\mathrm{sling}}\\
      &+
      \frac12
      \left[
        F_x(p_{xo}-p'_{xo}S)
        +
        F_y(p_{yo}-p'_{yo}S)
      \right]\\
      &-
      \frac12
      \left[
        U_{xx}\epsilon_x(\alpha_x+\gamma_xS)
        +
        U_{yy}\epsilon_y(\alpha_y+\gamma_yS)
      \right].
\end{aligned}
}
$$

This familiar uncoupled expression is a specialization of the full
covariance-matrix formula, not a separate rule.

## 9. Sliced soft-Gaussian strong-strong specialization

Consider a field particle in beam 1 and a source slice in beam 2. Define

$$
    S_1=\frac{z_1-z_{2,*}}{2},
    \qquad
    u_2=-S_1.
$$

Let the source-slice transverse moments at its reference plane be

$$
    \overline{\mathbf r}_2,
    \qquad
    \overline{\mathbf p}_2,
    \qquad
    A_{2,0},
    \qquad
    B_{2,0},
    \qquad
    Q_{2,0}.
$$

Linear transport to the collision point gives

$$
    \mathbf C_2(u_2)
      =
      \overline{\mathbf r}_2
      +
      \overline{\mathbf p}_2u_2,
$$

$$
    A_2(u_2)
      =
      A_{2,0}
      +
      u_2(B_{2,0}+B_{2,0}^T)
      +
      u_2^2Q_{2,0}.
$$

Thus

$$
    \mathbf C_{2,u}
      =
      \overline{\mathbf p}_2,
    \qquad
    A_{2,u}
      =
      B_{2,0}+B_{2,0}^T+2u_2Q_{2,0}.
$$

With the paraxial Hirata virtual drift, the directed kick on the field
particle is

$$
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
    \Delta p_{z,1}
      ={}&
      \frac{
        (p_{x,1}^+)^2+(p_{y,1}^+)^2
        -(p_{x,1}^-)^2-(p_{y,1}^-)^2
      }{4}\\
      &+
      \frac12\mathbf F_1\cdot\overline{\mathbf p}_2\\
      &+
      \frac14H_{U,1}:
      \left(
        B_{2,0}+B_{2,0}^T+2u_2Q_{2,0}
      \right).
\end{aligned}
}
$$

In laboratory components, the covariance line is

$$
    \frac14
    \left(
      U_{xx}a_{2,u}
      +
      2U_{xy}b_{2,u}
      +
      U_{yy}d_{2,u}
    \right).
$$

In principal-axis components, the same line is

$$
\begin{aligned}
    &\frac14
    \left(
      \widehat U_{11}\lambda_{1,u}
      +
      \widehat U_{22}\lambda_{2,u}
    \right)\\
    &\qquad
    -
    \frac12\theta_u
    \left(
      \widehat F_x\widehat y
      -
      \widehat F_y\widehat x
    \right).
\end{aligned}
$$

The reverse directed kick is obtained by exchanging beams 1 and 2 and using
the collision coordinate and source moments appropriate to the opposite
direction.

If all cross-plane covariances vanish, then $b=b_u=\theta_u=0$, and the
strong-strong covariance term reduces to

$$
    \frac14
    \left(
      U_{xx}\frac{d\sigma_x^2}{du}
      +
      U_{yy}\frac{d\sigma_y^2}{du}
    \right).
$$

For a rigid Twiss ellipse this is exactly the weak-strong hourglass term
derived in Section 8.2.

## 10. Correctness checks and limiting cases

The derivation provides several independent checks:

1. **No transverse kick.**
   If $\mathbf F=0$, then $p_x^+=p_x^-$ and
   $p_y^+=p_y^-$, so the slingshot term vanishes.

2. **Constant source centroid.**
   If $\mathbf C_u=0$, a static offset changes the sampled transverse field
   but produces no separate centroid longitudinal term.

3. **No hourglass or covariance transport.**
   If $A_u=0$, the entire covariance contribution vanishes.

4. **Uncoupled source.**
   If $b=b_u=0$, the mixed-Hessian and rotation terms vanish.

5. **Fixed tilt.**
   A nonzero constant $\sigma_{xy}$ may rotate the transverse field, but if
   $\theta_u=0$, there is no rotation-derivative longitudinal term.

6. **Changing tilt.**
   If $\theta_u\ne0$, the term
   $$
       -\frac12\theta_u
       (\widehat F_x\widehat y-\widehat F_y\widehat x)
   $$
   is required. The invariant check is that it combines with the eigenvalue
   terms to reproduce $\tfrac14H_U:A_u$.

7. **Round covariance.**
   When $\lambda_1=\lambda_2$, the angle is arbitrary. The laboratory-frame
   contraction remains the nonsingular reference expression.

8. **Weak-strong/strong-strong agreement.**
   Substituting
   $\operatorname{Cov}(x,p_x)=-\epsilon\alpha$,
   $\operatorname{Var}(p_x)=\epsilon\gamma$, and $u=-S$ into the
   strong-strong moment formula reproduces the weak-strong Twiss formula.

9. **Six-dimensional symplecticity.**
   For a complete map with Jacobian $J_{\mathcal M}$,
   $$
       J_{\mathcal M}^T\Omega J_{\mathcal M}=\Omega.
   $$
   A finite-difference Jacobian with nonzero centroid angle, curvature,
   $b_u$, and $\theta_u$ is a direct numerical check of all derivative
   terms.
